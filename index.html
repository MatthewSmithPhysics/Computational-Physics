What is This Page?

This page documents my journey to self-learn computational physics. It is to serve as evidence to any employer or institution that I have experience in computing in the context of physics and maths. Although knowledge in this field is no doubt helpful in my studies, my programme does not directly support and assess this, so I feel that this page's existence is necessary.

What Resources am I Using?

I own a copy of Computational Physics by Thijssen. This is a fairly advanced textbook, which assumes a fair amount of undergraduate physics knowledge which I simply don't possess. I am thus keeping that one on standby until I have learned about the topics covered in that book.

For the first few entries in my log, I have been using K.F. Riley's Mathematical Methods for the Physical Sciences as a guide for what to study. I have mainly been writing the code for various numerical methods and also doing things like convergence tests to understand them.

As of January 2019, I own a copy of Sastry's Introductory Methods of Numerical Analysis . This should be helpful in studying specific techniques, particular those in linear algebra and ODEs/PDEs.

D For technical subject knowledge, I have been using textbooks from my formal courses.

What Experience do I Have Going In?

As of writing (26/06/2018), I have just completed my first year of a Maths and Physics degree at Durham University. I have taken the courses Analysis I, Calculus and Probability I, Linear Algebra I, Foundations of Physics 1, and Discovery Skills in Physics (lab work). I have been programming since 2013 and am fairly confident in using Java, Python, C++, and Fortran. In terms of using this for physics and maths, I have created various simulations of physical systems for presentations to a student-run Physics Society in sixth-form and am working on my own linear algebra C++ library, which is hosted on another account's repository (I may port it onto this one).
